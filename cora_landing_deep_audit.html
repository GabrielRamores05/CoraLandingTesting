Critical bugs found in your actual HTML
Bug #1 — Fatal
Modal auto-opens on page load, blocking everything
Your body has class="cora_modal_open" and style="overflow: hidden" hardcoded. So when any visitor lands, the booking modal immediately pops up before they read a single word. This is conversion suicide — you're asking for commitment before giving value.
✦ Fix: Remove cora_modal_open from body's default class. Only add it when user clicks "Book a Demo." Let people see your page first.
Bug #2 — Fatal
Navbar has only 1 link ("Services") — no product explanation
Your navbar has a "Services" anchor link and "Contact Us" CTA. No About, no Pricing, no How It Works. Visitors landing from ads have no way to self-educate before deciding. They leave.
✦ Fix: Add: Features · How It Works · Pricing (even if "Contact for pricing") · Partners
Bug #3 — Fatal
Hero logo is 160px tall — takes up 40% of above-the-fold space
Your .hero_logo is 160px height with 40px margin-bottom. On mobile, your entire hero above-the-fold is a giant logo, eyebrow text, and the H1. The description and CTA are below the fold. You lose visitors before they reach your CTA.
✦ Fix: hero_logo max 48px. Move logo to navbar only. Use that real estate for your value proposition headline.
Bug #4 — Fatal
Stats show "0" — all stat counters start at 0 and may not animate
Both the hero stats and partners stats show 0 as their initial value. The JS counter animation relies on IntersectionObserver but your HTML has style="opacity: 0" on the stat numbers — if the animation script fails or is slow, visitors see "10+", "0+", "0+" which destroys credibility.
✦ Fix: Always hardcode the real number as fallback. Use JS to animate UP from 0, but set the actual number as the default DOM content.
Bug #5 — High
Two conflicting CTAs in hero: "See How It Works" vs "Get Offline Cora"
Your hero has two equal-weight buttons with no visual hierarchy. Visitors freeze when given two choices of equal importance. The "paradox of choice" kills conversion — they click neither.
✦ Fix: One PRIMARY button (filled orange: "Book Free Demo"). One GHOST/text link below it: "or explore offline edition →"
Bug #6 — High
Booking form asks for 7 fields upfront — massive friction
First Name, Last Name, Email, Cooperative Name, Cooperative Type (15 dropdown options!), Role (6 options), Mobile, Demo Date, Facebook. That's 9 required fields. Every extra field costs you ~11% conversion. You're asking for everything before they've decided to trust you.
✦ Fix: Step 1 — just "Cooperative Type" tile selection (zero typing). Step 2 — Name + Email + Mobile only. Collect the rest after they confirm via email.
Bug #7 — High
Footer is invisible — has opacity:0 in inline style
Your footer element has style="...opacity: 0" baked in from a scroll animation that apparently hasn't fired. Same for footer columns. Your contact info, privacy links, and social proof at bottom are literally invisible without JS.
✦ Fix: Never set initial opacity:0 via inline style on critical content. Use a CSS class that JS removes/adds.
Bug #8 — Medium

Only Facebook has a real link. LinkedIn, Google, and Instagram all point to "#" — which means clicking them scrolls to the top. This signals to visitors that your brand isn't fully established yet.

Bug #9 — Medium

Your <head> has charset, viewport, and title "cora-landing-page" (not even a real title). No meta description means Google won't index you properly and social shares will look broken.

Bug #10 — Low
carousel_track is tripled but the animation calculation may break
Your logo carousel uses the same 7 logos 3 times (21 items). The animation uses calc(-100% / 3) to loop. This is fragile — if logos load at different widths, the loop position can jitter visibly.
✦ Fix: Use a JS-powered infinite carousel or clone dynamically. The CSS-only hack breaks on mobile.

Psychological weapons to add — what big companies actually do
Loss framing (most powerful)
People hate losing something 2.5× more than they love gaining the same thing. Frame your copy around what they LOSE by NOT using CORA.
Instead of "Manage records easily" → "Stop losing ₱50,000/year to manual errors and missed penalties"
Social proof cascade
Stack 3 types: numbers ("10+ coops"), logos (your 7 partners), and testimonials. Each type convinces a different type of skeptic. You currently have only logos.
Add: "Join 1,000+ cooperative members already using CORA daily" near the top
Commitment & consistency
Once someone takes a small action (clicking a button, selecting a coop type), they're psychologically driven to complete the process. Multi-step forms use this — step 1 is so easy there's no reason to stop.
First interaction: "What type of cooperative are you?" [4 tiles] — before ANY form fields appear
Authority & legitimacy
Cooperatives are regulated. Your buyers (board chairs, treasurers) are risk-averse. Signals of official legitimacy remove the biggest objection: "Is this real/safe?"
Add: "CDA-compliant · SEC-registered · Trusted by FACCS Federation" near your hero CTA
Scarcity & urgency (real)
You literally have 2 demo dates coming up. That's REAL scarcity — use it. A countdown timer to the next available demo slot creates genuine urgency without fabricating anything.
"Next demo: Thursday June 19 · 3 of 5 spots remaining" — countdown live on the page
Reciprocity (give first)
Give something genuinely useful before asking for anything. A free tool, checklist, or calculator makes visitors feel obligated to reciprocate with their contact info.
"Free Download: CDA Annual Report Checklist 2025 — no email required" (then ask after they click)
Risk reversal
The fear of making a bad decision is the #1 reason people don't click. Remove the risk explicitly: free demo, no contract, no installation, money-back guarantee.
Under your CTA: "Free · No contract · No installation needed · Cancel anytime"
Specificity = credibility
Specific numbers are more believable than round ones. "10+ coops" feels generic. "14 cooperatives across Bicol" feels real and verified.
Change "10+ cooperatives" → "14 cooperatives across Region V" (if true). Specific beats generic every time.
